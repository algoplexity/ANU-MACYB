What must change:

1. Define “regime” formally.

Not metaphorically.

For a dynamical system:

xₜ₊₁ = F(xₜ, θ)

A regime is a region in parameter space Θ where qualitative invariants of F remain stable.

Without that, “regime” is rhetorical.

2. Define “mesoscopic” operationally.

Mesoscopic cannot mean “between micro and macro” in prose.

It must correspond to:

Features invariant under micro-noise but sensitive to parameter drift.

For example:

• spectral structure
• entropy rate
• causal graph topology
• transfer entropy network
• compression slope

Otherwise it is branding, not theory.

3. Separate instrumentation from philosophy.

Viable System Model, complexity economics, AI fluency — these are framing layers.

Your thesis core must be one of:

A) An observability theorem
B) A representation-learning principle with MDL penalty
C) A control-stability proof under regime-aware adaptation

If you try to prove all three, the work fragments.

4. Avoid the “universal transducer” trap.

Your earlier discussion already revealed the limit:

No fixed representation is universal.

Your doctoral-level contribution could be:

A bounded search over representations penalized by description length that yields stable mesoscopic invariants.

That is mathematically defensible.

5. Hardware is a means, not the contribution.

The CPS is valuable because:

• You control the ground-truth regime parameter.
• You can induce controlled transitions.

If the hardware does not provide parameter ground truth, it weakens the thesis.

Not regime detection.
Not transformers.

A formal bridge between representation choice and regime observability under complexity constraints.

Theorem: A representation E preserves regime identifiability iff it retains sufficient statistics for parameter θ under bounded noise.

Adaptive MDL-based encoding approximates this condition under resource constraints.
