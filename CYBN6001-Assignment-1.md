## 1. Executive Summary

This plan outlines an 80-minute facilitation session designed to interrogate the cybernetic relationships between sensing, acting, and systemic coordination. Utilizing the "Algorithmic Mesoscope" (Coordination Viability Oscilloscope) as a central case study, the session bridges high-level computational theory (Algorithmic Information Theory) with embodied practice and Indigenous ways of knowing.

The session employs a PESTEL-informed cybernetic lens to evaluate how technological sensors and human agents form "hybrids" in complex networks. Key activities include a live Python simulation of the $C(t)$ Coordination Coherence Metric and a "Social Mesoscope" workshop where the cohort simulates bounded rationality and algedonic feedback. The session concludes with a high-order evaluation of how "kin-sensing" and algebraic connectivity can inform the design of safe, sustainable, and responsible cyber-physical systems.

## 2. Methodology and Framing

The session follows the ANU School of Cybernetics' "Cybernetic Wheel" model: **Sensing $\rightarrow$ Interpreting $\rightarrow$ Deciding $\rightarrow$ Acting $\rightarrow$ Reflecting**.[1]

### Cybernetic Lenses Applied:

* **Heterogeneity:** Analyzing how diverse agent inputs (human and sensor) contribute to systemic resilience.[1]
* **Connections:** Utilizing Actor-Network Theory (ANT) to view non-human agents (metrics and sensors) as active participants in social organization.
* **Plurality:** Integrating the Dharug concept of "kin-sensing" to challenge Western "reductive" views of sensing.
* **Feedback Loops:** Implementing algedonic signals (stress alerts) to maintain homeostasis in the "Social Mesoscope" workshop.

## 4. Facilitation Session Structure (80 Minutes)

### Part 1: The Hook – "Sensing the Invisible" (10 Minutes)

* **The Inversion:** Introduce the "Damian’s Car" case study but invert it. Ask: "If the lock is fixed, but the *coordination* between the driver and the car is broken, are you really inside the system?"
* **Key Message:** Stability is a "regime," not a scalar. We cannot manage what we cannot sense.[1]

### Part 2: Framing the Controversy – ANT & PESTEL (20 Minutes)

* **Debate:** Using the PESTEL framework, facilitate a discussion on who owns the "sensing" in a smart city.[1]
* **Question:** "Is a $C(t)$ metric a neutral observation or an active political actor that dictates how we are allowed to coordinate?".

### Part 3: The "Mesoscope" Workshop – Simulation & Embodiment (35 Minutes)

* **The Live Simulation:** Run the Python Coordination Simulator (see Immersive Code below) to show the transition between *Stable Alignment*, *Oscillation*, and *Fragmentation*.
* **Physical Activity:** Divide the cohort into 3–6 participant "nodes" per group.[1]
* *Task:* Coordinate a shared rhythm without verbal communication (Semiotics).
* *Feedback:* Facilitators provide manual "algedonic signals" (e.g., red cards) when the group rhythm fragments, simulating the CVO’s LED entropy signals.



### Part 4: Synthesis & Evaluation – Higher Order Thinking (15 Minutes)

* **Evaluation:** Move to HOTS.[1] Critique "smart buildings" using the Dharug "kin-sensing" lens.
* **Closing Question:** "How do we design systems that increase our algebraic connectivity to each other and the environment, rather than just optimizing individual output?".
